ART1

Katherine Nash (USA) and Richard H. Williams (USA), 1970

View PAGE 7 (PDF)

ART1 was one of the earliest computer art languages designed for direct use by artists. Developed in 1970 by Katherine Nash and Richard H. Williams, it ran on an IBM 360/40 with a high speed line printer, allowing users to create intricate character based designs without advanced programming skills.

Through simple coded instructions, artists could generate lines, rectangles, triangles, ellipses, and other geometric forms, apply repetition and symmetry, and overprint to create complex patterns. ART1 bridged the worlds of art and computing at a time when access to computers was rare and costly.

In the UK, Roger Saunders adapted ART1 for the ICL 1905 at Brighton College of Technology, with early works shown at the Bradford Art Festival. This collaboration between artists and engineers across continents marked a key moment in making computer technology an accessible creative tool.
